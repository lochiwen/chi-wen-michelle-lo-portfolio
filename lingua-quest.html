Project deep dive

Lingua Quest: A Language-Learning Game for Kids

A game-based learning concept for children ages 6-8 that combines vocabulary practice, pronunciation, cultural discovery, and a passport-style reward system to make early language learning feel playful, supportive, and meaningful.

View full report PDF

Project summary

Lingua Quest was designed for young learners who are beginning to connect language learning with family, relocation, heritage, school, and social belonging. The concept responds to a common challenge in early language learning: memorization alone is not enough to sustain attention, confidence, or cultural curiosity.

The game turns foundational vocabulary into a travel-themed learning journey. Children choose a language, learn topic-based words through mini-lessons, practice pronunciation, play a catching game, earn stars and passport stamps, and unlock interactive cultural videos that let them apply what they learned in context.

Product context

Audience
Children ages 6-8, especially lower-grade learners beginning a second language.
Core problem
Traditional vocabulary repetition can feel disconnected from play, culture, and real motivation.
Design medium
2D mobile and tablet game with touch interaction, voice input, and child-friendly visual feedback.
My contribution
Learner personas, content structure, cultural event mapping, storyboard flow, and second-language theory alignment.

Learning game loop

The experience is structured as a repeatable loop that gradually increases difficulty while keeping the child in a positive, low-pressure learning rhythm.

  1. Choose a language Learners select from eight languages, including Portuguese, Mandarin, Spanish, Hindi, English, French, Italian, and Japanese.
  2. Pick a topic and level Each topic contains six levels, with one new word added at a time and earlier words reviewed.
  3. Learn with a mini-lesson New vocabulary appears with visuals, native-language support, target-language audio, and pronunciation modeling.
  4. Practice pronunciation Children repeat the word aloud and receive supportive feedback before entering the game activity.
  5. Play the catching game Learners catch the image that matches the word spoken aloud, connecting listening comprehension to visual meaning.
  6. Earn stars and stamps Completed levels earn stars; completed topics earn passport stamps and unlock cultural discovery content.
  7. Unlock cultural missions Videos and missions invite children to apply vocabulary inside cultural scenarios, such as a celebration or local tradition.
  8. Review through progression Later levels include previously learned words, creating a built-in review path and summative practice.

Content scope

The curriculum focuses on everyday vocabulary children can recognize, say, and reuse in family or school contexts. Each topic contains six words and becomes progressively more challenging.

  • Colors: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, purple.
  • Shapes: circle, square, rectangle, triangle, hexagon, oval.
  • Food: egg, milk, water, banana, bread, carrot.
  • Family: dad, mom, brother, sister, grandpa, grandma.
  • Emotions and feelings: happiness, sadness, anger, fear, surprise, disgust.

Learner needs

The personas represent children learning language for different emotional and social reasons: moving to a new country, communicating with grandparents, reconnecting with heritage, or visiting extended family.

  • Support confidence for children who feel shy, anxious, or afraid of pronunciation mistakes.
  • Use music, cartoons, movies, and playful media habits as bridges into language learning.
  • Connect vocabulary to culture so language feels socially meaningful, not just academic.

Design rationale

The report connects the game mechanics to multimedia learning, second-language acquisition, affective design, motivation, sociocultural learning, and evidence-centered assessment.

Scaffolded difficulty

Each new level adds one word while retaining prior vocabulary, helping children build confidence through manageable challenge.

Comprehensible input

Words are paired with audio, visuals, native-language comparison, and target-language modeling before gameplay begins.

Supportive feedback

Correct answers trigger positive reinforcement, while mistakes prompt encouragement and another attempt instead of harsh correction.

Culture as motivation

Passport stamps, travel narratives, and cultural videos turn progress into curiosity about places, traditions, and people.

Assessment strategy

Assessment is embedded in play rather than separated from it. Children demonstrate listening comprehension by matching spoken words to visuals, show pronunciation readiness before gameplay, and continue reviewing earlier words as levels progress.

  • Stars indicate level-level progress and performance.
  • Passport stamps indicate topic mastery.
  • Cultural missions assess transfer by asking learners to use vocabulary in a scenario.

Portfolio takeaway

Lingua Quest shows my ability to design for young learners, connect learning objectives to game mechanics, translate theory into product features, and create a curriculum structure that balances play, assessment, motivation, and cultural awareness.
