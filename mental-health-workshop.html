Project deep dive

Mental Health Workshop for Formerly Incarcerated Individuals

A learning design project focused on mental health literacy, healthcare navigation, self-reflection, and decision-making for adults recently released from incarceration.

View original project site

Project summary

The project began with a broad re-entry challenge: people leaving incarceration often need support around housing, employment, healthcare, education, and independence. Secondary research and an interview with attorney Tolu Lawal helped the team understand the re-entry context; after discussion with the professor, the team narrowed the scope to mental health literacy and healthcare navigation.

The final design is a three-day in-person conference with foundational sessions and interactive workshops. It helps learners understand mental health care, practice decision-making, explore stigma, build personal care plans, and connect with resources and community support.

Learning context

Audience
Adults recently released from incarceration and navigating re-entry into society.
Core problem
After release, healthcare systems can be complex, inaccessible, stigmatizing, and overwhelming.
Design medium
In-person multiday conference with required sessions, optional workshops, advisors, and printed support materials.
My contribution
Design ideation, workshop activity design, action-planning concept, team reflection, and learning experience framing.

Learning goals

The workshop series was designed to support both mental health knowledge and the social-emotional life skills needed to act on that knowledge after re-entry.

  1. Understand mental health care Explain roles in the mental health system, common diagnoses, symptoms, treatments, and available resources.
  2. Connect knowledge to experience Apply mental health and healthcare knowledge to personal history, including conditions that existed before incarceration.
  3. Challenge stigma Explore stigma around mental health disorders, recognize preventative care, and build empathy for others.
  4. Practice life skills Use self-reflection, goal-setting, and decision-making as active skills rather than abstract advice.
  5. Identify resources Find, use, and share appropriate mental health and healthcare resources for themselves and others.
  6. Make action plans Plan how to acquire and use tools, services, and support systems available after release.
  7. Build confidence Help learners feel more prepared to make informed decisions about their health care.
  8. Share safely Create a supportive space for learners to share experiences, receive resources, and build community.

Research insights

Research showed that people leaving incarceration often experience a sudden return of independence after a long period in which daily choices were highly controlled. That transition can make healthcare navigation, technology use, and personal decision-making especially difficult.

  • Mental health appeared as a recurring issue before, during, and after incarceration.
  • Incarcerated individuals experience disproportionate rates of mental health and substance use disorders.
  • During re-entry, mental healthcare and insurance must be navigated alongside housing, employment, and basic services.
  • Technology-heavy solutions could exclude learners who had limited access to modern tools while incarcerated.

Design direction

The team moved from a broad re-entry support concept to a mental health literacy conference because the learner needs were diverse, sensitive, and deeply connected to confidence, autonomy, and community support.

  • Required sessions provide shared foundational knowledge before learners make choices.
  • Optional workshops let learners practice autonomy without being overwhelmed too early.
  • Advisors, networking, and follow-up gatherings support human connection beyond a single session.

Conference experience

The selected approach was a three-day in-person conference that gradually moves from awareness to action, then into self-directed planning.

  1. Day 1: Awareness and understanding Introduce mental health, incarceration, systemic issues, stigma, and misconceptions in mental healthcare.
  2. Day 2: Action and skills Use panels, resource activities, and healthcare simulations to practice asking questions and navigating support.
  3. Day 3: Guided choice Let learners choose breakout sessions after they have a shared foundation and access to advisor support.
  4. Conference packet Provide schedules, course descriptions, note space, resource pages, and contact information in a printable format.
  5. Flexible logistics Use a later start, registration time, meals, and open advisor time to reduce transportation and attendance barriers.
  6. Community support Include networking and optional follow-up gatherings so learners can continue sharing and receiving support.

Workshop activities

The ideation process produced a set of practical activities designed to make mental health knowledge usable in real re-entry contexts.

  • Reentry mental health role-play simulation with realistic scenarios and resource searches.
  • Journal and mental health worksheet to support reflection, dialogue, and resource awareness.
  • Mental care action plan using concrete who, what, where, when, and how steps.
  • Follow-up gatherings for peer support, professional consultation, and community continuity.

Learning theories

The design draws on adult learning, situated learning, social constructivism, self-regulated learning, behaviorism, and collaborative learning.

  • Adult learning supports relevance, autonomy, and respect for learner experience.
  • Situated simulations let learners practice real-world problem solving in a safer environment.
  • Social constructivism supports shared meaning-making through discussion, panels, and peer exchange.
  • Self-regulated learning supports reflection, self-assessment, planning, and follow-through.
  • Behaviorism supports repeated practice, feedback, and concrete action steps for healthcare navigation.
  • Collaborative learning supports peer connection for learners rebuilding life after release.

Development iterations

The conference schedule went through several revisions. Each version helped the team make the experience easier to follow, less overwhelming, and more supportive for learners returning to everyday life after incarceration.

Version 1

The first schedule gave learners many choices, including keynotes and multiple optional course blocks each day. This supported autonomy, but it could also create too many decisions too early in the conference.

Version 2

The second version reduced the number of choices and moved most optional sessions to the final day. It also added advisor support, color-coded schedules, and networking time so learners had more guidance and community support.

Version 3

The final schedule organized the conference into clearer day themes: awareness, action, and guided choice. It used longer sessions, a later start time, registration time, breakfast, and open advisor hours to make attendance more realistic.

Materials

The team also developed a learner packet with the schedule, course descriptions, note space, resource references, and contact information, plus sample lesson plans to show how individual sessions could be taught.

Evaluation plan

The evaluation approach combines formative feedback during the conference with follow-up interviews after learners have had time to use the skills in daily life.

  • Daily exit surveys ask about interest, usefulness, satisfaction, and advisor support.
  • Workshop observations track engagement and immediate learner feedback.
  • Post-conference interviews examine retained skills, daily-life use, and missing topics.
  • Usability testing asks learners to review the course catalog and fill out their own schedule.

Portfolio takeaway

This project shows my ability to work on sensitive, justice-oriented learning problems, narrow a broad social challenge into teachable goals, and design an accessible learning experience for adults with complex life circumstances.

  • Translated research and interview insights into a focused mental health literacy learning experience.
  • Balanced learner autonomy with scaffolding, advisor support, and emotional safety.
  • Designed practical activities that connect knowledge to action, reflection, and community support.
